1. Pleasure travel motivation is often added to a business trip such as attending a convention. How do you sell this Idea to the convention planning committee?


Answer:

Having been to several conventions, there are two areas I would identify.

One, there is more to a convention than meetings and trade shows. There is networking. And the best way to meet people that you wouldn’t otherwise meet is on a non-business business activity. Many times this is an evening show or a side trip to a nearby attraction. If you want attendees to like you event, you take this into account.

Two, spouses and partners come along. A convention will likely take 3–5 days. Many times, significant others don’t want to be left behind for that long. So they take the trip also. While the attendee is in the convention activities, there needs to be something for the partners to do. And being in a place where there are activities is very conducive to a successful convention.

Finally, adding a weekend to a convention is a great way to relax after the hustle of the event. Being in a location to do that adds to how people feel about the convention.

Utilitarianism is one of the best known and most influential moral theories. Like other forms of consequentialism, its core idea is that whether actions are morally right or wrong depends on their effects. More specifically, the only effects of actions that are relevant are the good and bad results that they produce. A key point in this article concerns the distinction between individual actions and types of actions. Act utilitarians focus on the effects of individual actions (such as John Wilkes Booth’s assassination of Abraham Lincoln) while rule utilitarians focus on the effects of types of actions (such as killing or stealing).


26. Mill proposes that “higher” pleasures are those preferred by the majority of people. Do you agree that this is a good way of distinguishing between higher and lower pleasures? Can a well-informed majority prefer higher pleasures? ​


While I am no expert in the subject of Mill’s philosophy the answer for me is yes and even a well-informed majority will prefer higher pleasures.

So let us ask first, what is considered higher pleasures for Mills? There are arguments in the philosophical field of what exactly Mills meant when he said this but one of the interpretations is that a higher pleasure is the one people would prefer despite the quantity of lower pleasures. The arguments for definitions revolve around discussions of quantity and quality. In Mill’s book Utilitarianism, he said that:

“If one of the two [pleasures] is, by those who are completely acquainted with both, placed so far above the other that they prefer it, even though knowing it to be attended with a greater amount of discontent, and would not resign it for any quantity of other pleasure which their nature is capable of, [then] we are justified in ascribing to the preferred enjoyment a superiority in quality, so far out-weighing quantity as to render it, in comparison, of small account. “

Then by definition is pleasure then quantifiable? How does one even quantify pleasure? But a closer look asks of us, then by virtue of being preferred does it not mean that the one being chosen is of higher quality despite the quantity? This question is metaphysical and warrants a more rigorous discussion.

I think here Mill is saying whichever is the one chosen is what would we consider higher pleasure. Since we have difficulty even defining what is higher and lower with guidelines and standards, the question of whether this is a good way to distinguish between the two depends on one’s philosophical preference. For me, Mill’s way of distinguishing it is as good as it’s gonna get.

If a majority is well-informed then if we’re working within the framework of higher vs. lower pleasures then whichever way they choose they will be choosing higher pleasures.
