5. direction describe the characteristic of fresh fish and shellfish in terms of appearance (look) texture (feel)and odor (smeell) accomplish the activity in your activity notebook​


Answer:

Fresh fish is typically found in bodies of water such as oceans or lakes and usually has a clean and shiny appearance. Fresh shellfish, such as shrimp and oysters, usually have a color that helps them blend in with their surroundings in the ocean and typically have a hard and crunchy texture when tasted. In general, fresh fish and shellfish should have a clean and pleasant smell, not emanating from the smell of fat or any ingredients that cause irritation in the nose.

The texture of fresh fish should be soft and juicy when tasted, showing no signs of drying or blackening. In the case of shellfish, they should have a crunchy and hard texture, showing no signs of drying or blackening. In general, fresh fish and shellfish should have a delicious and satisfying taste, showing no signs of drying or blackening.

Overall, fresh fish and shellfish should have a clean and shiny appearance, a soft and juicy or crunchy and hard texture, and a delicious and satisfying smell and taste. By following these characteristics, a person can determine if the fish and shellfish are fresh or not and decide whether to buy it or not.

Fresh fish generally have a few distinct characteristics:

Appearance: Fresh fish typically have a smooth, moist skin that is free of blemishes or discoloration. The flesh should be firm and should not appear sunken or dried out. The eyes should be clear and not cloudy or sunken.

Feel: Fresh fish should feel firm to the touch, with a slight elasticity. It should not be slimy or slippery, as this may indicate spoilage.

Smell: Fresh fish should have a mild, slightly briny smell. It should not have a strong, fishy odor, as this may indicate spoilage.

12. Trees improve the atmosphere of a place by giving a feeling of freshness and coolness as they reduce temperature. *TrueFalse​


Answer:

true

Explanation:

Trees, with their canopies (the part comprising branches and leaves), block heat from reaching these surfaces. Tree canopies can intercept up to 90% of the sunlight—and the heat that comes with it. Some tree species with wider canopies, wider leaves, and more leaves are even more efficient. For example, beeches block up to 97% of the light that falls on them. Tropical trees with huge leaves are also super efficient, allowing only 1-2% sunlight to reach the ground. With less heat reaching below the tree’s canopy, the temperature stays low.

Technically, shade doesn’t cool the air. It just warms the air less.
