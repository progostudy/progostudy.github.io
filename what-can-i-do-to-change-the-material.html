Physical changes and Chemical changes

Light a fire on a candle because a lit candle will melt. It is called physical change because when it melts there is a change in the shape of the object from solid to liquid. There are two types of changes when a candle is lit, they are physical changes and chemical changes (when the candle wick burns). When the wood burns there is a chemical change.

Physical Changes Due to Changes in State

A physical change is a change in a substance's property that does not produce a new type of substance. Any material that undergoes a change of state due to the influence of heating will have the same properties. This phenomenon causes the material to return to its original nature. Some examples of changes in state are melting, melting, melting, evaporation, condensation, freezing, sublimation, and others.

The candle that goes through the melting process due to heating, causes the candle to change its shape, which was initially from a solid to a liquid. Likewise, when the molten wax is left to stand at a certain temperature, it will change its form into a solid again, but with an imperfect shape as before.

Chemical change

A chemical change is a change in a substance that produces a new type of substance. Chemical changes are changes that are permanent. Examples are combustion, decay, and rust/corrosion. In addition to physical changes, candles can also change through chemical changes, by going through the combustion process, which is caused by the presence of flammable gases close to combustible materials.

Chemical changes due to combustion

Basically, combustion itself is a chemical reaction that occurs between the burning material and oxygen gas in the air. In addition to producing ash and gas, combustion can also produce energy. As happens to wood that is burned, it will produce light energy around it.

Answer:

A chemical change (chemical reaction) is a change of materials into other, new materials with different properties, and one or new substances are formed. Burning of wood is a chemical change as new substances which cannot be changed back (e.g. carbon dioxide) are formed. For example, if wood is burned in a fireplace, there is not wood anymore but ash. Other examples include burning of a candle, rusting of iron, baking a cake, etc. Special details that describe how a chemical change takes place are called chemical properties.

Compare: Physical change - The opposite of a chemical change is a physical change. Physical changes are a change in which no new substances are formed, and the substance which is changed is the same. For instance, if a stick of wood is broken, there is still a stick of wood; it is just broken.

More examples include changes of shape, changes of states, passing electricity through a copper wire, breaking of wood, shattering of glass, pouring of water, etc. Special details which do not change in a substance without new substances being formed are called physical properties.

24. what can I do to change the material candle?what changed happened to the material​


The Change in Compositions of CandleWhen you light a candle, the heat from the flame melts the wax near the wick. This liquid wax is pulled up the wick by capillary action.  

The heat of the flame vaporizes the liquid wax (turns it into a hot gas) and begins breaking the hydrocarbons into hydrogen and carbon molecules. These vaporized molecules are drawn into a flame where they react with oxygen in the air to produce heat, light, water vapor (H2O), and carbon dioxide (CO2). About a quarter of the energy produced by burning a candle is released when the flame radiates heat in all directions.

Enough heat is generated and radiated to melt more wax to continue the combustion process until the fuel runs out, or the heat is removed.

When you light a candle for the first time, it takes a few minutes for this burning process to stabilize. The flame may flicker or smoke a little at first, but once the process stabilizes, the flame burns cleanly and evenly, forming a steady teardrop, and releasing carbon dioxide and water vapor.

A quietly burning candle flame is a highly efficient combustion engine. However, if there is too little or too much air or fuel in the flame, it will flicker or flicker and unburned carbon particles (soot) will escape from the flame before it is completely burned. The plumes of smoke that are sometimes seen during candle flickering are actually caused by unburned soot particles that have escaped the flame due to incomplete combustion.
