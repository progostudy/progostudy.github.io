5. Directions. Think of your own ways of preventing cigarette smoking and alcoholism. Then answer the following questions as your guide and affix your signature on your work and ask your parents to read and assign. Write your answer on a separate sheet of paper. 1. as a teenager what can i do to prevent alcoholism and cigarette smoking?. ______________________________________________ 2. how can i help my classmates, schoolmates and my family not to smoke and drink alcohol excessively?. ______________________________________________​


Answer:

Focus on short-term consequences. As adults, we know that tobacco can lead to life-threatening illnesses such as heart disease, emphysema, and lung cancer. But most kids don’t worry about long-term risks. They can’t imagine what it’s like to be older or to be sick. Make sure you also focus on the immediate consequences of smoking. Kids don’t like bad breath, smelly clothes, yellow teeth, or poor performance in sports, which can all be caused by using tobacco.

Run a reality check. Kids tend to overestimate the number of children their age and older who engage in all sorts of risky behaviors, including smoking, using drugs, and drinking alcohol. Make sure your child knows that the large majority of high school students don’t smoke.1

Talk about peer influence. Point out how this things will influence your them. (It’s easier to say no to a stranger who asks you to drink alcohol instead of a friend.) Acknowledge some of the tough situations for them may face, and suggest positive ways of dealing with them. Remember, too, that positive peer pressure can help keep  them away from tobacco, alcohol, and other drugs. Talk about classmates and friends admires who don’t engage in these behaviors.

Point out the costs. Figure out together how much it would cost each year to smoke and how many hours it would take, at typical teen wages, to earn that. Then talk about what else your kids might do with that money.

Set the rules. Tell  them the consequences for using tobacco in your family. If you discover your child is using tobacco, make sure you follow through on the consequences you set.

7. Activity 3. BE A PART OF CHANGE Direction: Write your commitment in your journal or activity notebook. Affix your signature on your work and ask your parents to read then sign. Use the following questions as your guide. Guide questions: 1. As a teenager, what can I do to prevent the spread of diseases? 2. How can I help my classmates, schoolmates, and my family not to be infected by diseases?​


Answer:

As a teenager, there are several things you can do to prevent the spread of diseases:

Wash your hands regularly with soap and water for at least 20 seconds, especially before eating or touching your face.

Cover your mouth and nose with a tissue or your elbow when you cough or sneeze.

Avoid touching your face, especially your eyes, nose, and mouth.

Practice physical distancing by staying at least 6 feet away from others and avoiding large gatherings.

Wear a face mask when around others or in public places.

Stay home if you feel sick or have been in contact with someone who has tested positive for a disease.

By following these guidelines, you can reduce your risk of getting sick and help prevent the spread of diseases to others.

To help your classmates, schoolmates, and family not be infected by diseases, you can:

Encourage them to follow the same guidelines you are following, such as washing their hands, wearing a mask, and staying home if they are sick.

Remind them to get vaccinated against preventable diseases like the flu.

Make sure they have access to reliable information about how to stay healthy and prevent the spread of diseases.

Offer to help them with tasks that may require leaving the house, such as grocery shopping, to reduce their exposure to others.

Be a good role model by practicing healthy habits yourself and encouraging others to do the same.

By working together and taking simple steps to prevent the spread of diseases, we can all help keep our communities healthy and safe.
