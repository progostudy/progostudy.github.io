22. 1. A photographer is taking pictures of a bride and groom and their 10 attendants. If she takes 4 people in a group, excluding the bride and the groom, how many different groups can she photograph?​


Step-by-step explanation:

There are 45 different groups that can be photographed. This is calculated by taking the total number of attendants (10) and subtracting the bride and groom (2), leaving 8 people. Then, calculate the number of combinations of 4 people from 8, which is 8!/(4!*4!) = 70/8 = 8.75 = 9. Finally, multiply 9 by the number of possible groupings (5) to get 45 different groups.

28. 2. A photographer is taking pictures of a bride and a groom, together with 6 sponsors. How many ways can he arrange the 8 people in a line if the bride and groom stand in the middle?​


Answer:

Seven people, no restrictions: 7! = 7 * 6 * 5 * 4 * 3 * 2 * 1 = 5040

Seven people, bride and groom together: Treat the bride and groom as one group. Now you have six “objects” to arrange: the five individuals and the bride/groom group. There are 6! = 720 ways to do that.

Now, for each of those 720 groups, there are two possibilities: the bride then the groom and the groom then the bride. That makes the total possible arrangements 720 * 2 = 1440.

Seven people, bride and groom not together: I’m having a hard time finding an elegant solution to this one. All I’ve been able to come up with so far is a brute force approach: Put the groom in the first chair then count the number of arrangements for the others. Put the groom in the second chair then count the number of arrangements for the others. Do this for all possible positions of the groom then add up the results.
