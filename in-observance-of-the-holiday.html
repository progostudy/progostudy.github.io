1. subdivision of Islam and the holidays & important observances of Islam​


Islam is divided into two major sects: Sunni and Shia. The majority of Muslims worldwide are Sunni, while the Shia are the second-largest branch of Islam.

The Sunni and Shia sects have some theological and ritual differences, although they share many fundamental beliefs and practices. The main difference between them is related to the question of who should succeed Prophet Muhammad as the leader of the Muslim community. The Sunni believe that the leader should be elected by the community, while the Shia believe that the leader should be a direct descendant of Prophet Muhammad.

In terms of holidays and important observances, Muslims around the world celebrate the following:

1. Eid al-Fitr: This holiday marks the end of Ramadan, the month-long period of fasting and spiritual reflection. It is celebrated with prayer, feasting, and exchanging gifts.

2. Eid al-Adha: This holiday commemorates the willingness of Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) to sacrifice his son as an act of obedience to God. It is celebrated with prayer, the sacrifice of an animal (typically a sheep or goat), and the distribution of meat to the poor.

3. Mawlid al-Nabi: This holiday commemorates the birth of Prophet Muhammad. It is celebrated with prayer, recitation of the Quran, and other religious activities.

4. Ashura: This holiday commemorates the martyrdom of Imam Hussein, the grandson of Prophet Muhammad. It is observed with fasting, prayer, and mourning.

5. Laylat al-Qadr: This is the Night of Power, the night on which the first verses of the Quran were revealed to Prophet Muhammad. It is observed with prayer and other religious activities.

6. Ramadan: This month-long period of fasting and spiritual reflection is one of the Five Pillars of Islam. Muslims fast from dawn until sunset and engage in additional prayers and spiritual practices during this time.

7. Hajj: This is the annual pilgrimage to Mecca, Saudi Arabia, which is one of the Five Pillars of Islam. It is a mandatory religious duty for Muslims who are physically and financially able to make the journey.

These holidays and observances are important to Muslims around the world and provide opportunities for spiritual reflection, community building, and acts of charity and generosity.

How a Jewish history reflected in the observation and holidays of Judaism?

You'd need a very long answer to cover everything but here are some examples:

(reverse order)

The Fast Days :

(The Ninth of Av which was this week, 10th of Tevet, 17th of Tammuz, Fast of Gedalya, Fast of Esther…) all mark great calamities that befell the Jewish People through history. Primarily the destruction of both Jerusalem temples and the start of our dispersions and diaspora. But also other terrible times.

Our prayers:

All the Jewish prayers today correspond to rituals and sacrifices that occurred when The Temples stood. The prayers themselves are full of yearning for Israel, Jerusalem and the restoring of the House of David, the Temple and God's reign of the land.

The Festivals:

Hanuka commemorates the Triumph of the Maccabeans over the Greeks.

Purim recalls the miraculous saving of the Jewish community in Persian Empire (major at the time) from edicted genocide.

Passover commemorates the exodus from slavery in Egypt, the splitting of the Red Sea.

Sukkot (Tabernacles) commemorates the 40 years in the desert under God's providence.

Shavuot commemorates the Revelation of the Masses and receiving the Torah at Mt. Sinai

Our history is very much part of our religion. Our joint experience very much part of our heritage.

And also Israel Independence Day is celebrated by many as a religious holiday with special prayers of thanks to G-d who miracously gifted this generation with what 100 generations before us dreamed of and prayed for and died for.
