16. 1.) What's the difference between a Slide transition and a Slide animation? Explain why you need translations and animations in Slide presentation 2.) Think of at least 2 ways on how translations and animation help in the communications and learning when presenting using PowerPoint.


Answer:

1:Slide transitions are the animation effects that are applied to whole slide whereas animation effects are applied to objects (text, shape, picture etc.) on a slide. For a slide you can have only one transition effect while each object on the slide can have multiple animation effects.

2:First, let's understand why we would use animations in a PowerPoint presentation. There are two common reasons:

Animations help you control the pace of the delivery of your presentation.

They can enhance the appearance and interest of your slides.

The above is typically achieved by transitioning either full slides, or a number of elements on a slide.

The best animations have an aesthetic use that matches your message, as well as help you control the pace of your presentation. Typically, animations just for enhancing the appearance of your slides tend to become frustrating for your audience.

27. Describe the translation and transcription processes for dna/rna in the animal cell​


Answer:

The translation and transcription processes are both essential steps in protein synthesis in animal cells. Here is a brief description of each process:

Transcription:

The first step is transcription, where the DNA sequence in a gene is copied into a messenger RNA (mRNA) molecule. This process takes place in the nucleus of the cell. The enzyme RNA polymerase attaches to the promoter region of the gene and unwinds the DNA double helix. The polymerase then reads the DNA strand and creates a complementary mRNA strand by matching the nucleotides of the DNA with the appropriate RNA nucleotides (A-U, T-A, C-G, and G-C). The mRNA molecule then separates from the DNA template and leaves the nucleus, carrying the genetic information to the ribosomes in the cytoplasm.

Translation:

The second step is translation, where the information carried by the mRNA molecule is used to synthesize a specific protein. This process takes place in the cytoplasm of the cell. Ribosomes, which consist of RNA and protein, attach to the mRNA molecule and begin translating the codons (sequences of three nucleotides) on the mRNA into amino acids. Each codon codes for a specific amino acid, which is carried to the ribosome by a transfer RNA (tRNA) molecule. The tRNA has an anticodon that matches the codon on the mRNA, ensuring that the correct amino acid is added to the growing protein chain. As the ribosome moves along the mRNA molecule, new amino acids are added to the growing protein chain until a stop codon is reached. The newly synthesized protein is then released from the ribosome and may undergo additional modifications to form its final structure and function.

Overall, transcription and translation are tightly regulated processes that are critical for gene expression and protein synthesis in animal cells.
