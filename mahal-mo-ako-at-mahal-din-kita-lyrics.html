3. Explain in English"Wag mo akong mahalin dahil mahal kita,Mahalin mo ako dahil mahal mo ako"​


"Wag mo akong mahalin dahil mahal kita,Mahalin mo ako dahil mahal mo ako"

In English translation, this means "Don't love me because I lòve you, lòve me because you love me too."

EXPLANATION:

This line means that the speaker wants to be loved not by force. The speaker wants to feel love that is genuine and not because his partner doesn't want to hurt the speaker's feelings. In Filipino, walang napipilitan. What the speaker only wants is true love where there is no one being forced to love the other and the feeling is mutual.

29. what is mahal din kita in ilocano? ​


Mahal din kita in ilocano  is Ay-ayatenka atau Ipatpategka.

Iloko (varieties: Iluko, Iloco, Ilokano, Ilocano) is the third most spoken language in the Philippines and the mother tongue spoken by about 10,000,000 people. Out of 10,000,000 users, 7,700,000 use this Iloko language as the primary language and 2,300,000 use this language as a second language.

The Ilokano people are descendants of Austronesian speakers from southern China through Taiwan. Several families and clans arrived by means of viray or bilog which means boat. The term Iloko comes from i-, meaning "from", and luk, meaning "bay". , so Iloko means "people from the bay". The Iloko people also call themselves Samtoy, a shortening of the Iloko phrase saö mi ditoy, which means "our language here".

Iloko Literature

The Ilocano people's animistic past provides a rich backdrop for folklore, myth and superstition. There are many stories of good and evil spirits and beings. Its mythical formation revolves around the giant Aran and her husband Angngalo, and namarsua (Creator).

The heroic story of Biag ni Lam-ang (The Life of Lam-ang) is one of the indigenous stories that survived during colonialism, although now it is retold with a mixture of many foreign elements. He reflects the values ​​that are important to the people of Iloko; This is the story of a hero's journey with passion, loyalty, understanding, respect and ancestral and family ties.

The habits of the Iloko people evolved in living rituals, cultural feasts and oral history. These things are celebrated with songs (kankanta), dances (sala), poetry (daniw), riddles (murmurtia), proverbs (pagsasao), reciprocated words called bucanegan (after the author Pedro Bucaneg, and the equivalent of Balagtasan on tagalog people) and heroic stories.

Grammar

Ilocano language has a form with a predicate at the beginning of a sentence. That is, verbs and adjectives are located at the beginning of the sentence, accompanied by other parts of the sentence. The Ilocano language uses a very complex list of affixes and repetitions to define grammatical categories. Trying to learn simple basic words and their related suffixes will help form existing sentences.
