13. In the desert ecosystem coyote and rattlesnakes both eat the same type of Mouse as a primary part of their diets recently coyotes have been hunted and killed by people decrease in the overall coyote population how will this most likely affect the rattlesnake population over time


It will most likely increase the rattlesnake population because, the decrease in coyote population will result in a increase of the mouse population ,and will provide for rattlesnakes to lay more eggs because, they have a larger food source.

In this situation, it is most likely that the population of the rattlesnake will increase over time. The rattlesnake and coyote eat the same animal, mice. This interaction is what we call competition.

Competition is defined as the interaction between prganisms that utilize the same kind of resource. These resources include space, water, food, shelter, and nutrients. Competition can be further divided into two categories, namely intraspecies competition and interspecies competition.

Intraspecific competition involves competition among members of the same species. This usually occurs in organisms that are highly territorial. For example, two lion prides compete for the same prey in the African savanna. This is intraspecies competition because the lions in that same area are of the same species.

On the other hand, interspecific competition invovles competition among members of different species. Your question is a perfect example of interspecies competition among coyotes and rattlesnakes.

14. How does Coyote first escape Buffalo Bull


Answer:

Coyote escaped from Buffalo Bull by jumping into a river and swimming to the other side, while Buffalo Bull was unable to follow due to his poor swimming ability.

Explanation:

In Native American folklore, there are many stories about the trickster figure, Coyote, and his encounters with other animals, such as Buffalo Bull. One such story goes like this:

Coyote was being chased by Buffalo Bull, who was angry because Coyote had stolen some of his food. As Coyote ran, he came across a river and quickly jumped in. Coyote swam across to the other side of the river, but Buffalo Bull was not a good swimmer and could not follow him.

However, Buffalo Bull was still determined to catch Coyote, so he waited by the riverbank, hoping that Coyote would eventually come back to his side. Coyote, being the trickster that he was, had a plan to outsmart Buffalo Bull.

Coyote found a hollow log and filled it with hot coals from a nearby fire. He then rolled the log down into the river and swam over to Buffalo Bull's side. When Buffalo Bull saw the log floating in the river, he thought it was Coyote trying to cross over and jumped into the river to catch him. But as soon as he landed on the log, the hot coals burned him and he jumped back into the water, shouting in pain.

Coyote laughed and ran away, while Buffalo Bull was left nursing his burns. And that's how Coyote first escaped Buffalo Bull in this particular Native American folktale.
